## 4. What changed between the n=1 trial and the n=3 campaign

The n=1 trial (single episode, seed 950) reported baseline SLA compliance
of ~1.7–3.3%. **This did not replicate.** Across 3 seeds, baseline's real
compliance is **bimodal by seed**, not uniformly near-zero:

| Seed | baseline compliance (all 3 slices, identical within a seed) |
|---|---|
| 950 | 60.3% |
| 951 | **100.0%** |
| 952 | 60.7% |

Seed 951 happened to land baseline at full compliance; seeds 950/952 sit
around 60%. The trial's single episode (seed 950) evidently sampled the
lower mode, making baseline look far worse than its true average
behavior. **The corrected, honest finding**: baseline's static ratio is
**highly seed/traffic-timing-dependent** — sometimes adequate, sometimes
not — whereas every learned arm holds exactly 100.0±0.0% compliance in
**every one of 45 learned-arm episodes**, regardless of seed. The
*qualitative* conclusion (learned arms are more reliable than the static
baseline) is unchanged and in fact more interesting stated this way: the
value of learning here is consistency under variable conditions, not
rescuing an otherwise-always-failing baseline.

## 5. Anomaly adjudications (pre-registered decision rules applied to the real data)

### 5.1 `a2c_qoe` mass blocking — **VERDICT: SYSTEMATIC**

Per-episode block counts (`experiments/results/live_campaign/results_raw.json`,
`per_rep.a2c_qoe`), all 15 episodes across all 3 seeds:

| Seed | eMBB blocks/episode | URLLC blocks/episode | mMTC blocks/episode |
|---|---|---|---|
| 950 | 47, 38, 35, 45, 41 | 35, 36, 46, 37, 40 | 40, 46, 41, 38, 41 |
| 951 | 42, 45, 41, 47, 39 | 39, 38, 39, 45, 46 | 41, 37, 42, 28, 37 |
| 952 | 48, 43, 43, 49, 35 | 42, 43, 46, 39, 47 | 32, 34, 33, 32, 40 |

Every single one of `a2c_qoe`'s 15 episodes blocks 28–49 requests on
**all three slices simultaneously**. The control comparison, `dqn_qoe`
(same reward, same traffic, different algorithm), shows **exactly zero
blocks in all 15 of its episodes**. The pre-registered rule required
">10 blocks/episode on ≥2 slices, recurring in ≥1/3 of episodes across
≥2 seeds" for a SYSTEMATIC verdict — this data clears that bar by a wide
margin (100% of episodes, all 3 slices, all 3 seeds, magnitudes 3–5× the
threshold).

**Interpretation**: this is A2C-specific, not QoE-reward-specific —
`a2c_sla` (same algorithm, SLA reward) shows zero blocks, and `dqn_qoe`
(same reward, DQN) shows zero blocks. Only the A2C+QoE-reward combination
exhibits this. It plausibly connects to a previously-documented framework
finding (an earlier investigation on the prior rig noted A2C's tendency
toward over-rejection under certain reward/Lmax configurations) — this
campaign provides live, multi-seed confirmation that the instability is
real and reproducible, not a training artifact. Despite the heavy
blocking, `a2c_qoe` still achieves 100% SLA compliance and the *highest*
mean episodic reward among the two QoE-reward arms (0.388 vs. dqn_qoe's
0.318) — i.e., the eq.9 reward does not clearly penalize this behavior,
which is itself a finding worth a paragraph in the paper's discussion
(a reward-design tension, not a bug in the campaign).

### 5.2 URLLC inferred-MOS floor — **VERDICT: DOES NOT REPLICATE AS STATED; a different, real finding underneath**

The n=1 trial reported URLLC MOS "pinned at exactly 1.0" for all 4 learned
arms. At n=3, the real values are:

| Arm | URLLC MOS (mean ± std) | eMBB MOS (mean ± std) | mMTC MOS (mean ± std) |
|---|---|---|---|
| baseline | 1.241 ± **0.331** | 1.260 ± 0.025 | 4.648 ± 0.088 |
| dqn_sla | 1.495 ± 0.046 | 1.223 ± 0.000 | 4.778 ± 0.005 |
| a2c_sla | 1.661 ± 0.120 | 1.223 ± 0.000 | 4.772 ± 0.002 |
| dqn_qoe | 1.552 ± 0.058 | 1.223 ± 0.000 | 4.773 ± 0.001 |
| a2c_qoe | 1.660 ± 0.064 | 1.224 ± 0.000 | 4.767 ± 0.005 |

The literal "floor at exactly 1.0" was an n=1 artifact (that specific
episode/seed happened to sample the bottom of the range). The **real,
robust finding underneath** is different but still genuine and worth
reporting:
- **Both eMBB and URLLC show consistently low inferred MOS (~1.2–1.7 on a
  1–5 scale) across every arm including baseline**, while mMTC is
  consistently high (~4.6–4.8) across every arm. This is **overwhelmingly
  policy-independent** — eMBB MOS in particular is identical to 3 decimal
  places across all 4 learned arms (1.223 ± 0.000) — meaning it reflects
  the QoE mapper's calibration against this traffic profile, not
  anything a control policy can influence. This is consistent with (and
  now extends) the already-documented Stage-One finding that URLLC's IQX
  fit is weaker than eMBB's/mMTC's; live data now shows the *inferred
  score*, not just the calibration fit metric, sits low for both
  non-mMTC slices regardless of policy.
- **Baseline's URLLC MOS variance (std 0.331) replicates and is by far
  the largest of any arm/slice** (next-highest is a2c_sla's 0.120) —
  consistent with baseline's bimodal compliance (§4): the seed that gave
  baseline 100% compliance (951) plausibly also gave it better URLLC MOS
  that episode, while the other two seeds pulled both metrics down
  together. This ties MOS variance directly to the same seed-dependent
  static-ratio fragility already established via compliance, not a
  separate phenomenon.
- Learned arms show a modest, consistent URLLC MOS improvement over
  baseline's mean (1.50–1.66 vs. 1.24) with much tighter variance — a
  real but small effect, worth stating honestly as "modest" rather than
  inflating it.

**Discussion framing (not contradiction, as the handover anticipated):**
learned arms achieve ~100% SLA compliance (a backlog/loss-budget measure)
while URLLC/eMBB inferred MOS stays low and largely policy-invariant —
these are genuinely different signals (SLA compliance reflects the
learned policy successfully managing the ceiling; MOS reflects a
separately-calibrated quality inference that this campaign's traffic
profile and QoE mapper simply don't map to a high score for these two
slices). This is legitimate discussion material for the paper: meeting
the SLA budget is not the same as achieving high inferred QoE, and a
QoE-aware reward's job is exactly to surface that gap — which it does
here.

## 6. Claims supported / NOT yet supported

**Supported by this campaign's data (cite run IDs / this report's §2-§5):**
- All 4 learned arms (DQN/A2C × SLA/QoE reward) achieve 100.0±0.0% SLA
  compliance across all 3 slices, in every one of 45 episodes, with zero
  losses against baseline in any single slice×seed comparison (§2, §3).
- The static baseline's SLA compliance is real but seed-dependent
  (60–100%, bimodal), not uniformly poor — the value of the learned
  arms is consistency, not rescuing a uniformly-failing baseline (§4).
- The ceiling-nudging mechanism (Fig. 4/headline) is the causal driver:
  baseline's ceiling never leaves its static floor; learned arms actively
  ride the ceiling to the calibrated cap within ~10-15 steps and hold it
  (established in the n=1 trial, mechanism unchanged and re-confirmed
  qualitatively across all 45 learned-arm episodes in this campaign).
- `a2c_qoe` exhibits a systematic (not noise, not reward-mode-general —
  A2C-specific) mass-blocking behavior across all 3 slices in 100% of
  its episodes, while still achieving full SLA compliance and the
  highest mean reward among the QoE-reward arms (§5.1).
- Inferred MOS for eMBB and URLLC is low (~1.2–1.7/5) and overwhelmingly
  policy-independent; mMTC MOS is high (~4.6–4.8/5) and equally
  policy-independent. SLA compliance and inferred MOS are dissociated
  metrics in this campaign, not redundant ones (§5.2).
- Empirical ratio-to-PRB calibration and the Phase 1 contention gate
  (ceiling↓⇒backlog↑, ceiling↑⇒relief) are real, reproducible testbed
  findings independent of which arm is running (established pre-campaign,
  reaffirmed by every arm's Fig. 4-style ceiling trace).

**NOT supported / open (say so plainly):**
- Whether `a2c_qoe`'s blocking behavior is *desirable* or a *flaw* is not
  resolved by this data alone — it achieves the same SLA compliance and
  a higher QoE-mode reward than `dqn_qoe` despite blocking far more
  requests, which is a reward-design question this campaign surfaces but
  does not adjudicate.
- Whether URLLC/eMBB's low inferred MOS reflects a genuine property of
  this traffic profile vs. a QoE-mapper calibration limitation cannot be
  disambiguated from this data (no ground-truth subjective MOS was
  collected — see Limitations).
- n=3 seeds is not enough to characterize baseline's compliance
  distribution beyond "bimodal in this sample" — whether it is truly
  bimodal (vs. a wider continuous distribution under-sampled at n=3) is
  open; the journal-length follow-up should use more seeds specifically
  for the baseline arm if this distinction matters to the narrative.
- No claim is made about generalization beyond this specific traffic
  profile, this specific rig, or rfsim vs. over-the-air behavior.

## 7. Limitations (carried into the paper's Limitations section verbatim in spirit)

- **rfsim, not over-the-air.** Real protocol stack and scheduler, not real
  RF channel impairments.
- **Single shared desktop rig** (7.4 GB RAM, 8 cores), not an isolated
  telecom testbed. Two genuine crashes occurred in the vendored OAI
  source during this campaign (a segfault in
  `nr_ue_periodic_srs_scheduling`, `nr_ue_scheduler.c:1140`, and an
  `AssertFatal` in `lockGet_ul_iterator`, `nr_ue_scheduler.c:223`) after
  ~6h45m of continuous operation; both were root-caused (dmesg +
  addr2line + source reading, not patched — frozen vendored code) and
  resolved by a fresh rig restart, with the affected arm-seed pairs
  **redone from scratch**, not patched or topped up. Neither crash
  recurred on the fresh rig. This is disclosed, not hidden: it is a real
  property of running this class of RL-admission-control experiment on
  shared commodity hardware for many consecutive hours.
- **Single gNB, 3 UEs** (one per slice) — paper #2's multi-gNB
  load-balancing term is structurally inapplicable ($\rho\equiv1$) and
  not evaluated.
- **n=3 seeds.** Sufficient to see zero-loss, tight-variance results for
  the learned arms and a real (not noise) bimodal pattern for baseline,
  but not sufficient for anything beyond descriptive effect sizes — no
  p-values are reported, and none should be trusted at this n even if
  computed.
- **Inferred, not measured, QoE.** Per-slice MOS is a calibrated
  IQX+LSTM inference (validated against P.1203/ACR proxies in Stage One),
  not real end-user subjective scoring.
- **Fixed traffic profiles.** eMBB/URLLC/mMTC traffic shapes were pinned
  once (`experiments/configs/traffic_profiles.yaml`) and never varied
  across arms — appropriate for this campaign's paired-comparison design,
  but results should not be read as characterizing behavior under a
  different traffic mix without re-running.
